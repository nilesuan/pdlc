# OKRs (Objectives and Key Results)

**Question:** What are OKRs, where did they originate, and how are they structured in practice by the organizations that popularized them?

**Status:** Draft

**Last updated:** 2026-04-24

---

## 1. Definition

[VERIFIED] "OKRs stand for **Objectives and Key Results**, a collaborative goal-setting methodology used by teams and individuals to set challenging, ambitious goals with measurable results" ([OKR meaning, definition, example — What Matters](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/okr-meaning-definition-example) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[VERIFIED] Components ([What Matters, n.d.](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/okr-meaning-definition-example) (accessed 2026-04-24)):

- **Objective** — "An Objective is simply what is to be achieved, no more and no less."
- **Key Results** — "Key Results benchmark and monitor how we get to the Objective. Effective KRs are specific, time-bound, and aggressive yet realistic."

### 1.1 Structure: Objective with 3–5 Key Results

[VERIFIED] Per What Matters' "How Many OKRs Should You Have?" FAQ (Doerr's companion site to *Measure What Matters*), the recommended structure is "a maximum of three Objectives, each with 3-5 Key Results" with all fitting "on one or two pages." Simplified: "2-3 Objectives, 3-5 Key Results. Less is more." Each Key Result should be "specific, time-bound, and measurable," fitting "on one line" ([How Many OKRs Should You Have? — What Matters](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/how-many-okrs-to-have) (accessed 2026-04-24)). This is the "Objective + 3–5 Key Results" structure commonly attributed to *Measure What Matters*, verified here via Doerr's companion site rather than the book itself.

## 2. Origin

[VERIFIED] Per What Matters' historical account, the framework builds on Peter Drucker's Management by Objectives (MBOs), then evolved at Intel under Andy Grove who "rarely mentioned Objectives without tying them to 'Key Results,'" a term he coined. Grove originally called it "iMBOs" (Intel Management by Objectives) and introduced key distinctions: quarterly cycles instead of annual, and separation from compensation structures ([What Matters, n.d.](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/okr-meaning-definition-example) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[VERIFIED] What Matters' "Origin Story" article corroborates this lineage, noting Grove drew on "Peter Drucker's Management by Objectives (MBOs), introduced in the 1950s" and that Grove "transformed [Intel] using OKRs," with revenues growing "from $1.9 billion to $26 billion" under his leadership; John Doerr "learned Grove's methods as a seminar student" and is described as "the 'Johnny Appleseed of OKRs'" who "introduced the philosophy to Google's founders in 1999" ([The Origin Story — What Matters](https://www.whatmatters.com/articles/the-origin-story) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[VERIFIED] John Doerr adopted Grove's framework and "introduced it to Google's founders in 1999." "Doerr was the one who crafted the name 'OKRs.'" He presented it "Gathered around a ping-pong table which doubled as a boardroom table" to Larry Page, Sergey Brin, and others. Google "set its company strategy with this management framework" ([What Matters, n.d.](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/okr-meaning-definition-example) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[VERIFIED] Andy Grove's book *High Output Management* (1983) is the canonical primary source for Grove's management-by-objectives practice. Wikipedia's entry (citing the book directly) states: the book "introduces Grove's 'management by objective' approach, also known as the objectives and key results (OKR) framework" ([High Output Management — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Output_Management) (accessed 2026-04-24)). Ben Horowitz's foreword to the 2015 Vintage Books edition describes Grove's management identity ("A manager's output = the output of his organization + the output of the neighboring organizations under his influence") and his teaching style but does not itself re-derive the OKR structure ([Andy — Ben Horowitz, 2015 (a16z)](https://a16z.com/2015/11/13/high-output-management/) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[SYNTHESIS] The lineage is therefore: Drucker (MBO, 1950s) → Grove (iMBO with Key Results, documented in *High Output Management* 1983) → Doerr (carried from Intel to Google, named OKRs, 1999, then *Measure What Matters* 2018) → broader adoption. This lineage is independently attested by What Matters (Doerr's site), Wikipedia's High Output Management entry, and Horowitz's a16z foreword.

## 3. Structure: committed vs. aspirational

[VERIFIED] Google divides OKRs into two contrasting types ([What Matters — Google OKR playbook excerpt](https://www.whatmatters.com/resources/google-okr-playbook) (accessed 2026-04-24)):

- **Committed OKRs** — "The expected score for a committed OKR is 1.0; a score of less than 1.0 requires explanation for the miss."
- **Aspirational OKRs** — "Aspirational OKRs have an expected average score of 0.7, with high variance" for goals where "we have no clear idea how to get there."

[VERIFIED] On structure and outcomes-focus, Google's guidance: Objectives "must be tangible, objective, and unambiguous," and Key Results "express measurable milestones which, if achieved, will advance Objective(s)" and "must describe outcomes, not activities" ([Google OKR playbook — What Matters](https://www.whatmatters.com/resources/google-okr-playbook) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[VERIFIED] The ambition principle: "Start with, 'What could my (or my customers') world look like in several years if we were freed from most constraints?'" — aspirational OKRs should extend beyond what existing resources comfortably permit ([Google OKR playbook — What Matters](https://www.whatmatters.com/resources/google-okr-playbook) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

## 4. OKRs vs. outcomes (Teresa Torres)

[VERIFIED] Torres characterizes OKRs as "a way to express an outcome." She explains "an OKR is comprised of an objective and key results," where the objective is qualitative and inspirational, while key results are quantitative measures ([Ask Teresa: OKRs vs. Outcomes — Product Talk, 2023](https://www.producttalk.org/2023/03/okrs-vs-outcomes/) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[VERIFIED] She distinguishes: "Business outcomes measure the health of the business. Product outcomes measure a change in customer behavior." Product teams can directly influence product outcomes but rarely control business metrics directly ([Torres, 2023](https://www.producttalk.org/2023/03/okrs-vs-outcomes/) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[SYNTHESIS] Practical implication for OKR design: a team-level Key Result written as a product outcome (a measurable behavior change) is more directly controllable than a business-metric KR. Torres's argument is that writing KRs as outputs ("ship feature X") is a failure mode the outcome-framing prevents.

## 5. Cadence

[VERIFIED] Grove's original innovation was "quarterly cycles instead of annual" ([What Matters, n.d.](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/okr-meaning-definition-example) (accessed 2026-04-24)). This quarterly cadence aligns with Cagan's observation that product strategy is "explicitly revisit[ed] every quarter" ([Mind the Product — Cagan interview](https://www.mindtheproduct.com/product-vision-and-strategy-marty-cagan-on-the-product-experience-part-1-of-2/) (accessed 2026-04-24)) and with SAFe's Planning Interval of 8–12 weeks ([Planning Interval — Scaled Agile Framework](https://framework.scaledagile.com/program-increment/) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

---

## 6. OKRs and compensation

[VERIFIED] Grove's other stated distinction: OKRs were "separat[ed] from compensation structures" ([What Matters — OKR meaning, definition, example](https://www.whatmatters.com/faqs/okr-meaning-definition-example) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[SYNTHESIS] This is an important operational detail: if stretch (aspirational) OKRs are expected to score ~0.7, tying them to bonuses incentivizes sandbagging — setting OKRs low so they can be met. Separating OKRs from compensation is therefore part of what makes aspirational targets functional.

## 7. CFRs (Conversations, Feedback, Recognition)

[VERIFIED] Doerr's *Measure What Matters* pairs OKRs with the CFR framework — "continuous performance management" intended to replace the annual review cycle. Per What Matters' CFR/OKR resource ([How do OKRs and CFRs Work Together? — What Matters](https://www.whatmatters.com/resources/difference-between-okr-cfr) (accessed 2026-04-24)):

- **Conversations** — "communications about work; progress, tactics, and potential bottlenecks"
- **Feedback** — "closes the gap between current actions and intentions; improving or changing performance or behaviors"
- **Recognition** — "positive, real-time acknowledgement for small or large wins, reinforcing what's working"

[VERIFIED] Relationship: "CFRs and OKRs complement each other, linking goals to a team's broader mission and strategies." What Matters frames the two via a soccer analogy: "Objectives as the goal lines – the targets. The Key Results are the yard markers that measure progress towards each goal." CFRs represent "the huddles, the line coaching, half-time pep talks – all the interactions that allow the team to adjust in real-time." Practically: "When CFRs are ongoing, they help you navigate your OKRs" ([What Matters, n.d.](https://www.whatmatters.com/resources/difference-between-okr-cfr) (accessed 2026-04-24)).

[SYNTHESIS] CFRs are the operational counterpart to OKRs. OKRs answer "what do we want?" across a quarter; CFRs answer "how do we stay on track?" on a daily/weekly cadence. Without CFRs, aspirational OKRs score poorly because course-correction happens only at cycle boundaries.
